Brief — Product Brief

A working brief for building a prototype of a new Granola feature.
Reader: the engineer or AI agent who will build the demo.
Date: 2026-05-18 · Status: v2 (simplified, portfolio-based)
Working name: Brief. The feature might not need a name at all in product — it could simply be what Granola shows you before a meeting.

1.What it is, in one sentence

Before a meeting starts, Granola surfaces one thing worth knowing — drawn from every meeting in the org that led up to this one — and lets you carry it into the meeting with one click.

2.What it isn't

Not a new tab. Not a new section of the app. Not a chat feature. Not a popup. Not a setting. Not a dashboard of insights. Not something the user has to find, opt into, or configure.

3.The insertion point (one place, no new IA)

Today, when you click an upcoming meeting in Granola, the main canvas shows the meeting title and a "ready to record" empty state. We replace that empty state with the brief.

That's the entire surface. No sidebar changes. No menu additions. No icons in the chrome.

4.What the brief contains

Two parts, in this order:

The room (always shown)

1–2 lines per attendee. Role · recency · the single most relevant open thread (if any).

Sarah Chen — Head of Product · last met Tuesday · you owe her the Q2 numbers

The insight (only when there's something worth saying)

One org-knowledge insight, drawn from a portfolio of types Granola can surface (Section 5). With one or two micro-actions inline.

If Granola has nothing worth saying for a given meeting, only "the room" renders. Most meetings will be quiet. The ones with insight will be unmissable.

This is the deliberate design bet: quiet over comprehensive. A meeting brief that's always full of "insights" trains the user to ignore it. A brief that's quiet 70% of the time and lands hard 30% of the time becomes something they actually read.

5.The insight portfolio

Granola picks one insight per meeting from five categories. Each demonstrates a distinct facet of org knowledge that only meeting data can produce.

Category Example What it proves only Granola can know
A
What changed since this was scheduled		 "Two assumptions behind your Q3 launch date have shifted this week — eng capacity dropped to 1, and the customer fix landed Tuesday." Granola tracks decisions across time, not just at the moment of decision.
B
Who else cares about this		 "Pat from infra has been raising your exact concern in 6 prior meetings. They're not on this invite." Granola maps real expertise — the org chart hides this; meetings reveal it.
C
What they've been carrying		 "Adam has raised hiring scope frustration in 4 of his last 5 meetings. Likely to come up." Granola sees patterns across one person's meetings you weren't in.
D
Open loops between you two		 "Sarah asked you for the customer retention breakdown in your last 1:1. Status: not yet sent." Granola hears commitments both directions — including the ones never docs'd.
E
Where the org is diverging		 "Growth and Trust have both been discussing this feature this month — Growth treats it as retention; Trust treats it as safety. They haven't connected." Granola is the only tool in every room — sees parallel meetings going different directions.

The wow isn't any single category. The wow is that Granola picks the right one for this meeting. Sometimes it's a 1:1 and Granola surfaces an open loop. Sometimes it's a pricing review and Granola surfaces a divergence. The variety is the demonstration of org understanding.

6.Micro-actions (two lanes only)

Reveal — "Show me…"

Inline expands a 3-line summary in place. Inside the expanded summary, a quiet link: "Open full note →" for users who want to go deeper. Keeps the user in the prep flow by default; never navigates away unless they ask.

Prep — "Bring this up"

One-click drops a pinned bullet at the top of the meeting note, marked as from your brief. The bullet stays pinned for the duration of the meeting.

Critically, the same one-click action carries through three beats — see Section 8 (The full arc).

What we deliberately avoided

  • Calendar moves (adding people to invites) — last-minute adds are socially expensive.
  • Auto-sends of any kind — drafts must always be reviewed before they leave Granola.
  • Editing other people's meetings or agendas — implies access we don't have.
  • "Take action for me" automation — violates the augment-judgment principle.

7.Light outreach (one exception)

For category B (Who else cares) only: a secondary action — "Ping Pat for a quick read" — drafts a short message in your voice. Drafts, never sends. The user sees the draft, can edit, and sends manually. This is the only Brief action that touches the outside world, and even then only as a draft.

8.The full arc — one click, three beats

The Brief isn't a one-moment feature. The "Bring this up" action carries the same insight through the entire meeting lifecycle:

Beat When What the user sees
1. Prep Click upcoming meeting The Brief appears in the meeting canvas. User clicks "Bring this up".
2. Reminder Meeting starts (Zoom/Meet detected) Granola's existing recording notification surfaces the bullet as a reminder: "Bring up: [X]". Reuses existing notification chrome — no new surface.
3. In-meeting Note opens during recording The bullet sits pinned at the top of the note, marked from your brief, ready to reference mid-conversation.

The same insight, the same action, three places it shows up. This is the moment the prototype proves the Brief isn't a passive sidebar — it's a feature that follows you into the room.

9.The hero moment

"Click an upcoming meeting. Granola hands you one thing no other tool could know. One click carries it through the meeting — reminder when you join the call, pinned at the top of the note when you're in it."

This is the demo sentence. The pitch to Chris. The marketing line.

10.Why this — not three modules, not a sidebar tab

The previous iteration of this brief had three always-on modules and a new sidebar tab. We dropped both because they violated the same principle in different ways: they made the user look for the org intelligence Granola has. The Brief flips that — it surfaces one load-bearing thing at the moment the user needs it, then gets out of the way. Most meetings won't have a hero insight, and that's the point. When one appears, it lands.

11.Voice & tone

Granola's voice: human, terse, slightly understated. The Brief must inherit it.

  • "You owe Sarah Q2 numbers" — not "Outstanding action item."
  • "Pricing v3 now hinges on legal" — not "Update: pricing v3 status changed."
  • "Pat has raised this in 6 prior meetings" — not "Insight: matching expertise detected."
  • "You weren't there" — directly, no hedging.
  • "Bring this up" — not "Add to agenda items."

Sentence case in UI copy. Never enthusiastic punctuation. If a line reads like a SaaS product, rewrite it.

12.Visual fidelity (non-negotiable)

The previous mock failed because it improvised Granola's visual language from memory. Don't repeat that.

  • Source of truth: real Granola screenshots in /references/. If the folder is empty when you start, stop and ask before building any UI.
  • Hierarchy: the note/editor surface is the hero of Granola. The Brief replaces the empty state of that surface, so it must inherit the same visual register — sparse, paper-feel, warm. Not a dashboard. Not a card-heavy interface.
  • Density: Granola is spacious. The Brief is two parts (the room + maybe an insight). Lots of breathing room. No badges, no status pills.
  • Color: warm off-white background, near-black text, a single muted accent used very sparingly.
  • Typography: match Granola's actual scale from screenshots. Match the existing app, don't invent a new system.
  • Layout: vertical stack, single column, width matches the existing note column. The Brief should feel like a kind of note, not a separate UI primitive.
  • Recording notification (Beat 2): reuse Granola's existing recording-start notification visual. Only the copy changes — the chrome must be identical to today's Granola.
  • Pinned bullet (Beat 3): the bullet at the top of the note should feel like a Granola note bullet, with the lightest possible mark to indicate from your brief — a small label or a subtle accent in the left margin, nothing more.

13.Principles

  1. The Brief is for the moment before the meeting. The "Bring this up" follow-through extends into the meeting only because the same one-click decision is already made.
  2. Quiet over comprehensive. No insight is better than a weak insight. Empty Brief is a feature, not a bug.
  3. The graph is invisible. Surface answers, not structures. Never show people-clusters, networks, scores, or confidence percentages.
  4. Augment judgment; never auto-execute. Every action requires a click. No background sends.
  5. Every line of content is justified by data Granola actually has. No speculation, no soft predictions.

When a design decision is ambiguous, the principle above wins.

14.Data sources

Everything the Brief shows must derive from data Granola already has access to:

  • Calendar — attendees, recurring meeting context, time-since-last-meeting, upcoming meeting detection (Zoom/Meet).
  • Transcripts — what was said in prior meetings with these people / on these topics.
  • Other employees' meetings and calendars — for categories A, B, and E. This is Granola's structural advantage; the Brief leans on it.
  • Your own edits and chat history — for category D (open loops).

No external data sources. No CRM integration. No Slack scraping. No org chart import.

15.Demo flow (the only path the prototype must support)

Three upcoming meetings in the sidebar. The user explores them in this order:

  1. Click "Weekly 1:1 with Sarah Chen" (in 10 minutes). The Brief surfaces:
    • The room: Sarah Chen, one line of context.
    • Insight (category A): "Two assumptions behind your Q3 launch date have shifted this week — eng capacity dropped to 1, and the customer fix landed Tuesday."
    • Inline actions: "Show what shifted" (reveal) and "Bring this up" (prep).
  2. User clicks "Show what shifted" → inline expands a 3-line summary citing the two source meetings. Includes "Open full note →" link.
  3. User clicks "Bring this up" → button state changes to confirm; a quiet inline confirmation: "Saved to your Sarah brief."
  4. Simulate meeting start. A "Start recording" affordance (or auto-trigger) fires. Granola's recording notification appears in the corner of the screen, copy: "Recording started — bring up: Q3 launch assumptions". (Match the visual style of Granola's existing recording notification — see /references/.)
  5. The meeting note opens. The pinned bullet sits at the top: "Bring up: Q3 launch assumptions (two have shifted this week)", marked subtly as from your brief.
  6. (Demonstrate breadth) User clicks back to sidebar, opens "Pricing strategy review" (in 30 mins). Brief shows insight category B: "Pat from infra has been raising your exact concern in 6 prior meetings. They're not on this invite." Inline actions: "Show me Pat's notes" and "Ping Pat for a quick read".
  7. (Demonstrate breadth) User clicks "Roadmap sync" (in 1 hour). Brief shows insight category E: "Growth and Trust have both been discussing this feature this month — Growth treats it as retention; Trust treats it as safety. They haven't connected." Inline action: "Show the other thread".

Three meetings, three insight categories (A, B, E), one full three-beat arc demonstrated end-to-end. Total demo time: ~90 seconds.

16.Out of scope (explicit non-goals)

  • A sidebar tab, dedicated view, or any other home for the Brief outside the pre-meeting canvas.
  • Editing the Brief manually.
  • Sharing the Brief with attendees.
  • Settings, customization, layout options.
  • A "Brief history" or archive.
  • Proactive surfacing outside the pre-meeting click and the meeting-start notification.
  • Categories C and D (Open loops, What they've been carrying) — strong but not in this demo. Documented in the portfolio for context.
  • Mobile / responsive below 1024px.
  • Empty / loading / error states beyond what the demo flow requires.

17.Acceptance criteria

  • A Granola daily user, shown the prototype with no introduction, says "oh — Granola does this now?" Not "what's this new feature?" Bar is invisible integration.
  • The Brief composes from data Granola plausibly has — no fabrication that breaks the spell.
  • Every line passes the voice test in Section 11.
  • The Brief adds zero new pieces of chrome to the existing app.
  • The recording notification (Beat 2) is visually indistinguishable from today's Granola recording notification except for the appended "bring up" copy.
  • The pinned bullet (Beat 3) reads as a Granola note bullet, with the lightest possible marking to indicate origin.
  • All three insight categories (A, B, E) appear in the demo, demonstrating the portfolio's breadth — not just one trick.

18.Naming

Working name: Brief. Direct, Granola-tonal, doesn't oversell. Possible the feature ships with no name at all — it's just what Granola shows you before a meeting. Decide later; don't let naming gate the build.
